Urban water systems, infrastructure, environments, and communities under climate stress.

The UHR Lab studies how urban hydrosystems interact with climate risk, infrastructure systems, environmental change, community knowledge, and decision-making to support resilient, equitable, and sustainable cities.

Coupled Systems

What we do

Research for resilient and sustainable urban water futures.

Our work combines hydrologic modeling, geospatial analysis, agent-based modeling, system dynamics, network analysis, and community-engaged research to understand how cities can better prepare for flooding, infrastructure stress, environmental change, and uneven climate impacts.

Research pathway

Coupled urban hydrosystems: theory for resilient decisions in practice.

The lab advances theory and practice by studying how urban water and climate risks emerge from interactions among water, infrastructure, environmental conditions, community knowledge, behavior, and governance. We use these insights to develop theory-informed models, maps, and decision-support strategies that help cities strengthen resilience in practice.

01

Diagnose risk

Map where water, climate, infrastructure, and environmental stress interact.

02

Explain mechanisms

Use socio-hydrology, systems thinking, behavioral theory, and network governance to interpret why risks emerge.

03

Model feedbacks

Analyze how behavior, governance, infrastructure, and exposure shape outcomes over time.

04

Support decisions

Turn models, maps, and community knowledge into usable evidence for action.

Research

Three research themes

The UHR Lab investigates urban hydrosystems through three connected themes: climate risk and flood dynamics, sustainable urban water and green infrastructure, and community-centered governance and decision support.

NOAA / flood exposure

Theme 1

Climate Risk & Urban Flood Dynamics

This theme focuses on how flooding, climate hazards, infrastructure conditions, land-use change, and uneven exposure shape urban risk. It connects hydrologic modeling with spatial risk analytics and climate adaptation needs.

Related projects

Community-informed urban flood modeling

Integrating resident observations, local knowledge, and hydrologic-hydrodynamic modeling to improve the characterization of urban pluvial flooding.

Flood burden and protection gaps

Analyzing how expected flood losses, insurance gaps, public residual losses, and institutional burdens vary across communities and hazards.

Flood exposure and spatial risk analytics

Using GIS, flood hazard data, remote sensing, and spatial indicators to evaluate how climate risks are distributed across urban areas.

Hydrology Flood risk Spatial analysis Infrastructure performance
EPA / green infrastructure

Theme 2

Sustainable Urban Water & Green Infrastructure Systems

This theme examines how urban water and environmental systems can be managed to support sustainability, affordability, green infrastructure adoption, and long-term resilience in cities.

Related projects

Green stormwater infrastructure adoption

Modeling how household behavior, social influence, affordability, parcel feasibility, perceptions, and flood exposure shape GSI adoption.

Water affordability and conservation

Studying how conservation strategies and urban water policies affect affordability, efficiency, equity, and sustainability outcomes.

Environmental and infrastructure indicators

Developing neighborhood-scale indicators that connect stormwater infrastructure, environmental conditions, flood risk, and community vulnerability.

Theme 3

Community, Governance & Decision Support

This theme connects technical analysis with the people, organizations, and institutions that shape flood resilience and water management decisions. It emphasizes stakeholder perception, governance networks, local knowledge, and usable tools.

Related projects

Flood governance networks

Analyzing cross-sector collaboration, coordination patterns, and governance gaps among organizations involved in flood risk management.

Stakeholder perceptions and cognitive maps

Studying how stakeholders understand flood drivers, impacts, vulnerability, uncertainty, and adaptation priorities.

Decision-support maps and tools

Translating model outputs, spatial indicators, and community insights into visual and practical tools for planning and adaptation.

Governance Citizen science Stakeholder perception Decision support

Coupled systems approach

Why the three themes belong together

Flood risk, water sustainability, green infrastructure, governance, and community knowledge are treated as connected parts of a coupled urban hydrosystem. This framing helps the lab study not only where risks occur, but also how infrastructure, behavior, institutions, and decisions change those risks over time.

Tools & Data

Models, maps, datasets, and decision-support products

The lab develops computational and communication products that support research, planning, adaptation, and community-engaged decision-making.

Models

Hydrologic and hydrodynamic workflows, agent-based models for GSI adoption, flood burden typologies, and governance network models.

Maps

Flood exposure maps, burden regime maps, infrastructure vulnerability indicators, GSI adoption maps, and community-oriented spatial summaries.

Decision Support

Scenario tools, stakeholder summaries, interactive visualizations, and planning-oriented outputs for agencies, communities, and researchers.

Code and reproducible workflows

Future repositories may include Python, R, GIS, NetLogo, Google Earth Engine, and visualization workflows connected to lab projects.

Data availability

Datasets and model products will be shared when possible, subject to privacy, licensing, collaborator agreements, and community protection considerations.
